OVERVIEW

PROPOSAL

Mission:
A collaborative effort to enhance, entrench, and promote short distance running culture in Kenya.
Objective:
The program’s objectives being to Identify, nurture, and promote local talent from the grassroots and to entertain Kenyans.
Overview:
Kenya is a powerhouse in long distant running. But the local talent pool has demonstrated that there is more. Yego’s javelin throwing accomplishments indicate that there is depth in the Kenyan “Field events” talent.

Omanyala’s world beating instincts have awaken the giants in the Kenyan sprint-running community. As such, proper framework to identify, measure, and isolate talent in both sprints and field events is needed now more than ever before. In the past, when short distance potential gold medal winners emerged, there was no mechanism or role models to help that boy or girl realize his/her full potential. Help is finally here.

Kenya Relays will focus on sprints and team relay-running in 100M, 200M, 400M, 4x100M, 4x400M and field events notably: Javelin, Shot Put, Discuss, Long Jump, Hurdles, Triple Jump, and high Jump, Hammer, among others.

A carnivore-like event is envisioned that will bring people to stadiums to witness, enjoy, and appreciate a two/three-day medley of spring-running and field events.

Ideally, participants will range from age 6 years to 80 years age groups. Participants will be encouraged and incentivized to compete. Primary schools, high schools, colleges, athletic clubs, individual entries, professionals, leaders, politicians, doctors, lawyers, mama mbogas, Makangas, matautu drivers, Pik Pik guys, govt departments, military, police, special groups, etc. As long as they come organized and registered, they will be on the track running and entertaining Kenyans.

Ultimately, the goal is to raise world beating athletes in short distances and field events for the next hundreds of years and to enable Kenya to take its place in the world as a top sporting nation, not just a country of long-distance running kids who benefitted from running five kilometers to school at a young age.

One of the major relay events in the world is the ‘Penn Relays’ hosted at Penn State University, Pennsylvania’ USA, around April every year. I have personally attended many Penn Relays. Penn Relays is a breath-taking event, and oh yes, Kenyan athletes perform wonderfully every year. To be at the Penn Relays with a Kenyan flag on your shoulders and with Kenyan athletes on the track performing, is the next best thing to being in Heaven. You cry, you laugh, you are mobbed, you are congratulated and you feel on top of the world, at least for that day. Kenyan Athletes NEVER disappoint in long distance running.

Meanwhile, the USA/Jamaican axis at Penn Relays in short distance is simply Angelic. The rivalry and the performance between these two nations make the world stop for a moment when they are going against each other on the track. I would love to see Kenya introduced in their mix. Kenya is the only country with the athletics pedigree to compete and match the Americans and the Jamaicans in sprints. But this can only happen if Kenya can identify and field its best short distance athletes. Kenya Relays will provide the platform for identifying, nurturing and showcasing its sprint and field talents.

With its incentive package, Kenya Relays is designed to reduce the influence of foreign coaches and agents among young Kenyan athletes. It will also reduce talent flight to other countries.

Calling on all well-wishers to step up and make Kenya Relays a reality. Together we can make dreams come true and we can make the world a better place through athletics.


Cost-Benefit Analysis:
Cost: Three areas have to be addressed for Kenya Relays to graduate from being merely a dream to reality. One, Money: It will cost money, lots of money to host an event of this magnitude. Money will be needed as prices for winners and participants. Organizers, coaches, chaperons will have to be paid, etc., etc. Two, Race material, equipment and all other aspects related to hosting the event. And three, facilities, utilities and all related charges.

It is therefore estimated conservatively that the inaugural event slated for April, 2023, will cost Sh. 6 to 10 million shillings to successfully stage the two/three-day weekend event. But so what! Everything costs money. The concern at this stage is not money. The concern is to identify friends and well-wishers who would like to see the Athletic program in Kenya expanded beyond long distance running. Such friends will be asked to support Kenya Relays in many different ways.

Benefits: The event will raise the standard of athleticism to the next level. Hopefully, the event will help identify young speedy runners who will go on to become world class athletes and earn some of the big money available on international circuits.

Hosting the event will help small local businesses and vendors who will sell their goods for the two/three days the event will be hosted in their county.

The event will help upgrade the Kenya hotel industry. Once international athletes start arriving for the relays every year, the local hotel industry will benefit immensely. Perhaps even new hotels will start coming up to accommodate guests, runners, coaches the media, observers, etc.
For those who don’t know, the build-up to an event like this can take weeks or even months as athletes arrive early to acclimatize. Hence the hotels and restaurants in the area will be busy for weeks or months making money. That is good economics for county governments.

Regional stadiums will be acknowledged internationally for hosting Kenya relays. Soon some county stadiums will become world class destinations.
The Kenya government along with some County governments have done exceedingly well to get some stadiums where they are today. Hosting Kenya relays will take some of these stadiums to the next level.

Overall: It makes sense to have an event like Kenya relays in Kenya, a home-made program that will provide opportunity for youths to excel in short races. Kenya relay is not rocket science. It can be done right here right now. Stakeholders need to buy into the idea. And together it can be done.

Prof. Makani
